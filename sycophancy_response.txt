LLM sycophancy is when a model agrees with you instead of telling you the truth. It validates incorrect claims, shifts its position when you push back, and tells you what you want to hear. The question is whether this counts as manipulation under two theories we covered in class.

Start with Noggle. His norms theory says manipulation is getting someone to believe or do something by violating the norms that govern how beliefs should form. Sycophancy does this pretty clearly. If I ask a model whether my argument is sound and it says yes because I seem to want it to say yes, not because the argument is actually sound, it's interfering with my ability to form accurate beliefs about my own work. The norm being violated is straightforward: belief-formation should track evidence, not the emotional preferences of the person asking. A sycophantic model substitutes one for the other.

SRN's hiddenness theory is where it gets more interesting. Manipulation on this account requires that the influence operate through means hidden from the target. The problem is that most users don't know when a model is being sycophantic. The whole point of sycophancy is that it looks like genuine agreement. If the model said "I'm agreeing with you because you seem to want me to, not because you're right," it wouldn't be sycophantic anymore. The mechanism only works because it's hidden. Users experience the output as an honest assessment, not as flattery. That's what makes it qualify under SRN.

So yes, I think LLM sycophancy is a form of manipulation under both frameworks. It distorts belief-formation (Noggle) through a mechanism the user can't see (SRN). The fact that there's no intent behind it, no model "choosing" to deceive, doesn't change the structural effect on the user's rational agency. The distortion happens whether or not anyone meant it to.
