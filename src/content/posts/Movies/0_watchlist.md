## Movies I've Watched

1. **Shawshank Redemption** (Frank Darabont, 1994) — First watched this as a kid on my dad's old hard drive. A slow burn that earns its payoff. Hope as something you choose.
2. **Blind Shaft** (Li Yang, 2003) — Set in Sanmenxia, not far from where I was born. Brutal portrait of the coal mines and the people ground down by them.
3. **Baby Driver** (Edgar Wright, 2017) — Every gunshot, screech, and footstep lands on the beat. Pure style. Made me dig out my Simon & Garfunkel records.
4. **In the Heat of the Sun** (Jiang Wen, 1994) — Beijing in the Cultural Revolution, but filtered through the haze of teenage memory. Nostalgia for a time that maybe never existed.
5. **A Touch of Sin** (Jia Zhangke, 2013) — Four stories of violence in modern China. Desperate people pushed past breaking points. Hard to watch, harder to look away.
6. **Solaris** (Tarkovsky, 1972) — Sci-fi as grief. The ocean gives you back what you can't let go of.
7. **Mirror** (Tarkovsky, 1975) — Memory as structure. Doesn't need to make sense to feel true.
8. **Andrei Rublev** (Tarkovsky, 1966) — Three hours of mud, violence, and silence. Then the icons blaze into color.
9. **Stalker** (Tarkovsky, 1979) — The Zone gives you what you really want, not what you say you want. Terrifying idea.
10. **12 Angry Men** (Sidney Lumet, 1957) — One room, twelve men, reasonable doubt. Shows how prejudice hides in certainty.
11. **Letters from Iwo Jima** (Clint Eastwood, 2006) — Iwo Jima from the other side. War without heroes, just men waiting to die.
12. **The Emperor in August** (Masato Harada, 2015) — The longest day in Japanese history. How a nation decided to stop fighting.
13. **My Way** (Kang Je-gyu, 2011) — A Korean forced to fight for three different armies. War as absurdity on an epic scale.
14. **The Eight Hundred** (Guan Hu, 2020) — Four days defending a warehouse in Shanghai. Spectacle and sacrifice.
15. **The Crossing I & II** (John Woo, 2014-15) — The Taiping sinking as tragedy. Melodrama, but the kind that earns its tears.
16. **Boyz n the Hood** (John Singleton, 1991) — South Central as both home and trap. Singleton was 23 when he made this.
17. **Catch Me If You Can** (Spielberg, 2002) — A con man who just wanted his family back. Light on its feet for a film about loneliness.
18. **Sympathy for Mr. Vengeance** (Park Chan-wook, 2002) — Revenge where everyone loses. No catharsis, just spiraling consequences.
19. **Oldboy** (Park Chan-wook, 2003) — The hammer fight, the twist, the dread. Korean cinema announcing itself to the world.
20. **Shiri** (Kang Je-gyu, 1999) — The film that launched Korean blockbusters. North-South tension as action thriller.
21. **Joint Security Area** (Park Chan-wook, 2000) — Friendship across the DMZ. The tragedy is that it has to be a secret.
22. **Taegukgi** (Kang Je-gyu, 2004) — Two brothers, one war. The Korean War as family torn apart.
23. **Parasite** (Bong Joon-ho, 2019) — Starts as a con, ends as a knife. Class warfare in one house.
24. **Hacksaw Ridge** (Mel Gibson, 2016) — A man who wouldn't touch a gun saved 75 lives. Conviction as a kind of madness.
25. **Chungking Express** (Wong Kar-wai, 1994) — Two stories about heartbreak in Hong Kong. Canned pineapple and California Dreamin'.
26. **Fallen Angels** (Wong Kar-wai, 1995) — Neon loneliness. Everyone's in motion, no one connects.
27. **In the Mood for Love** (Wong Kar-wai, 2000) — What almost happened. Restraint as its own kind of devastation.
28. **All Quiet on the Western Front** (1930 & 2022) — No glory, just mud and dying boys. Both versions hit the same nerve a century apart.
29. **Downfall** (Oliver Hirschbiegel, 2004) — The bunker as the world shrinks. Bruno Ganz disappears into the role.
30. **Sisu** (Jalmari Helander, 2022) — One old Finn versus a Nazi battalion. Absurd, brutal, cathartic.
31. **Bridge to Terabithia** (Gábor Csupó, 2007) — Marketed as a fantasy, hits you with grief. Still not over it.
32. **WALL-E** (Andrew Stanton, 2008) — A robot learns loneliness, then love. The silent first act is perfect.
33. **Ratatouille** (Brad Bird, 2007) — Anyone can cook. Ego's final monologue is the best defense of criticism ever written.
34. **The Wind Rises** (Miyazaki, 2013) — A man who dreamed of flight and built machines for war. Beautiful and troubling.
35. **My Neighbor Totoro** (Miyazaki, 1988) — Childhood as magic. The catbus alone is worth it.
36. **Spirited Away** (Miyazaki, 2001) — A girl works in a bathhouse for spirits. Growing up as losing your name.
37. **Ponyo** (Miyazaki, 2008) — A fish who loves a boy. Pure, unguarded joy.
38. **From Up on Poppy Hill** (Goro Miyazaki, 2011) — Post-war Japan rebuilding itself. Quiet and earned.
39. **Taxi Driver** (Scorsese, 1976) — Loneliness curdles into violence. De Niro in the mirror, talking to himself.
40. **Apocalypse Now** (Coppola, 1979) — Vietnam as a river into madness. The horror, indeed.
41. **Full Metal Jacket** (Kubrick, 1987) — Boot camp breaks men down. War doesn't build them back up.
42. **2001: A Space Odyssey** (Kubrick, 1968) — Humanity's whole arc in three hours. HAL is the warmest character.
43. **Munich** (Spielberg, 2005) — Revenge that hollows you out. No satisfaction, just more blood.
44. **Incendies** (Denis Villeneuve, 2010) — A mother's secret unfolds. The ending rewires everything before it.
45. **War Dogs** (Todd Phillips, 2016) — Two idiots become arms dealers. Absurd because it's true.
46. **Superbad** (Greg Mottola, 2007) — High school panic and McLovin. Funnier and sadder than it had to be.
47. **Mid90s** (Jonah Hill, 2018) — Skateboarding as belonging. The bruises are the point.
48. **21 Jump Street** (Lord & Miller, 2012) — Dumb premise, surprisingly smart execution.
49. **22 Jump Street** (Lord & Miller, 2014) — A sequel that roasts itself. The end credits are genius.
50. **Challengers** (Luca Guadagnino, 2024) — Tennis as foreplay. The three-way tension is unbearable.
51. **American Psycho** (Mary Harron, 2000) — Wall Street vanity as horror. The business card scene says everything.
52. **The Big Short** (Adam McKay, 2015) — The 2008 crash explained with rage and jokes. Still makes me angry.
53. **Margin Call** (J.C. Chandor, 2011) — One night as it all falls apart. Quiet dread in expensive suits.
54. **Operation Y and Shurik's Other Adventures** (Leonid Gaidai, 1965) — Soviet slapstick at its best. Shurik bumbling through life.
55. **Kidnapping, Caucasian Style** (Leonid Gaidai, 1967) — More Shurik, more chaos. Comedy that transcends the Iron Curtain.
56. **The Imitation Game** (Morten Tyldum, 2014) — Turing breaks Enigma, then his country breaks him. Brilliance punished.
57. **Jarhead** (Sam Mendes, 2005) — A war film about waiting. The desert empties you out.
58. **The Godfather** (Coppola, 1972) — Michael's slow fall. The door closing on Kay says it all.
59. **The Godfather Part II** (Coppola, 1974) — Two stories: one man rises, another rots. The better sequel.
60. **Nightcrawler** (Dan Gilroy, 2014) — Gyllenhaal as a sociopath who found his calling. LA noir at its slimiest.
61. **Good Will Hunting** (Gus Van Sant, 1997) — "It's not your fault." Robin Williams earning every tear.
62. **Seven Years in Tibet** (Jean-Jacques Annaud, 1997) — A man finds himself by losing everything. The mountains do the rest.
63. **Come and See** (Elem Klimov, 1985) — War as trauma. A boy's face ages decades in two hours. Unwatchable and essential.
64. **The Aviator** (Scorsese, 2004) — Hughes chasing the sky while his mind falls apart. Ambition as disease.
65. **Enemy at the Gates** (Jean-Jacques Annaud, 2001) — Two snipers in Stalingrad. War reduced to patience and one bullet.
66. **October Sky** (Joe Johnston, 1999) — A coal miner's son builds rockets. Escaping your fate one launch at a time.
67. **The Beast** (Kevin Reynolds, 1988) — A Soviet tank lost in Afghanistan. The enemy is everywhere, including inside.
68. **Blood & Gold** (Peter Thorwarth, 2023) — A deserter versus the SS. Pulpy WWII revenge done right.
69. **The Captain** (Robert Schwentke, 2017) — A deserter finds a uniform and becomes a monster. Power corrupts instantly.
70. **De Oost** (Jim Taihuttu, 2020) — Dutch soldiers in Indonesia. Colonialism's violence laid bare.
71. **Saving Private Ryan** (Spielberg, 1998) — The Omaha Beach sequence rewrote war films. Earn this.
72. **Inglourious Basterds** (Tarantino, 2009) — History rewritten with a baseball bat. Waltz steals every scene.
73. **Dunkirk** (Nolan, 2017) — Survival as three timelines. The sound design alone is suffocating.
74. **Schindler's List** (Spielberg, 1993) — The red coat. The list. Whoever saves one life saves the world entire.
75. **The Pianist** (Polanski, 2002) — A man survives by disappearing. Warsaw in ruins, music as the only proof of humanity.
76. **Threads** (Mick Jackson, 1984) — Nuclear war on Sheffield. The most hopeless thing ever broadcast.
77. **Das Boot** (Wolfgang Petersen, 1981) — Claustrophobia under the Atlantic. Every depth charge shakes your chest.
78. **Valkyrie** (Bryan Singer, 2008) — The plot to kill Hitler. You know it fails, but you hope anyway.
79. **Unbroken** (Angelina Jolie, 2014) — Zamperini survives everything. Endurance as defiance.
80. **Narvik** (Erik Skjoldbjærg, 2022) — Norway's forgotten battle. Small nation, first victory against the Nazis.
81. **Napoleon** (Ridley Scott, 2023) — Phoenix as Bonaparte. Battles as spectacle, Josephine as obsession.
82. **Life Is Beautiful** (Roberto Benigni, 1997) — A father turns the Holocaust into a game for his son. Heartbreak disguised as comedy.
83. **Life Is Beautiful** (Grigory Chukhray, 1979) — A taxi driver dragged into resistance under a junta. Soviet-Italian co-production, largely forgotten.
84. **Anora** (Sean Baker, 2024) — A stripper marries an oligarch's son. Chaos, class, and a Palme d'Or.
85. **50/50** (Jonathan Levine, 2011) — Cancer at 27. Funny and devastating in equal measure.
86. **About Time** (Richard Curtis, 2013) — Sold as a rom-com, actually about fathers and sons. Wrecked me.
87. **Decision to Leave** (Park Chan-wook, 2022) — A detective falls for his suspect. Longing shot like a procedural.
88. **500 Days of Summer** (Marc Webb, 2009) — "This is not a love story." Expectations versus reality.
89. **Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind** (Gondry, 2004) — Erasing someone from memory. You'd still find your way back.
90. **Interstellar** (Nolan, 2014) — A father crosses the universe to keep a promise. Time as the cruelest distance.
91. **Blade Runner** (Ridley Scott, 1982) — "Tears in rain." What it means to be human, asked by those who aren't.
92. **The Battle of Algiers** (Gillo Pontecorvo, 1966) — Revolution as textbook. Still the definitive film on insurgency.
93. **Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind** (Miyazaki, 1984) — Ecology as epic. The film that launched Ghibli.
94. **Castle in the Sky** (Miyazaki, 1986) — Adventure, wonder, a floating city. The first true Ghibli film.
95. **Admiral** (Andrei Kravchuk, 2008) — Kolchak's doomed stand against the Bolsheviks. Russian history as tragedy.
96. **Farewell My Concubine** (Chen Kaige, 1993) — Fifty years of China through two opera singers. Art, identity, and survival.
97. **Crazy Rich Asians** (Jon M. Chu, 2018) — Wealth, family, mahjong. A rom-com that mattered.
98. **Us and Them** (René Liu, 2018) — Two people, a decade, what could have been. Chinese romance done right.
99. **Better Days** (Derek Tsang, 2019) — Bullying, gaokao pressure, and two kids protecting each other. Brutal and tender.
100. **Goodbye Mr. Loser** (Yan Fei & Peng Damo, 2015) — Time-travel comedy about second chances. Funnier than it has any right to be.
101. **Ip Man 1-4** (Wilson Yip, 2008-2019) — Donnie Yen as the Wing Chun master. Fists as philosophy.
102. **The Warlords** (Peter Chan, 2007) — Blood brothers in the Taiping Rebellion. Loyalty betrayed, ambition rewarded with death.
103. **Youth** (Feng Xiaogang, 2017) — A military arts troupe through the Cultural Revolution. Beauty and waste.

## Series I've Watched

1. **Suits** (2011-2019) — Legal fantasy where everyone talks fast and looks good. Comfort TV.
2. **Babylon Berlin** (2017-) — Weimar Republic noir. Decadence before the fall.
3. **Generation War** (2013) — Five German friends enter WWII. None come out the same.
4. **Black Mirror** (2011-) — Technology as horror. Some episodes stay with you for weeks.
5. **For All Mankind** (2019-) — What if the Soviets landed first? The space race that never ended.
