---
title: "How We Got Here"
pubDate: "2026-03-15"
---

I was the kid who got picked last in gym class. Not metaphorically. Literally, consistently, across every sport, every year, from grade 4 through grade 10. I had the coordination of someone who had recently been informed that they had limbs. Volleyball was a recurring humiliation. Basketball was worse. I once got hit in the face with a basketball, which I didn't think was physically possible until it happened to me.

The thing about hating phys ed is that it's not really about fitness. It's about the specific social horror of being visibly bad at something in front of thirty people who are grading you on a curve you didn't agree to. I developed an entire personality around being the "academic kid" partly because it was true and partly because it was a convenient excuse to never run.

---

Then grade 11 happened. I needed something to do with the nervous energy my brain was producing in industrial quantities, and running turned out to be the cheapest available option. The first run was maybe 2 km and I walked half of it. But I went again the next day, and the day after that, and at some point it stopped being a coping mechanism and started being a thing I just did.

I also started lifting. The calisthenics came first: pull-ups, dips, push-ups, the stuff you can do in a basement with a doorframe bar. Then a gym membership. Then three years of consistent strength training, which gave me the upper body of someone who lifts and the lower body of someone who had never heard of leg day. This would become relevant later.

---

By grade 12 I was racing. Not well, but racing. A few 5Ks turned into a half marathon, which turned into two more half marathons, which turned into a full marathon, which in hindsight was too much too fast but that's a pattern you'll notice. I wasn't fast. I was stubborn. There's a difference, but the finish line doesn't care which one got you there.

I was also swimming a lot, and somewhere in there I got on a bike and didn't get off for a while. Century rides became normal. Then I did a 220 km race, which is the kind of distance where you stop counting kilometres and start counting gas stations. The three disciplines were accumulating independently. It was only a matter of time before someone pointed out there was a sport that combined all of them.

The first triathlon was a Subaru-sponsored sprint in Milton. Then Muskoka Ironman 70.3. A 1.9 km swim in a lake, a 90 km bike through Muskoka hills, and a 21.1 km run to finish. This is the triathlon equivalent of saying "I should learn to cook" and then signing up for MasterChef. I have no idea what possessed me. I finished. That's the nicest thing I can say about my time.

But something clicked. The thing I liked about endurance racing, the thing that made it different from every sport I'd been bad at in gym class, was that it rewards consistency over talent. You don't need to be fast. You need to show up six days a week for months. I can do that. I have OCD. Repetitive behaviour is sort of my thing.

---

After Muskoka I got serious. Not "I'll train more" serious. "I will learn exercise physiology and periodization and build structured training blocks" serious. The kind of serious where you own a spreadsheet with your heart rate zones colour-coded and you know what lactate threshold means and you say things like "cruise intervals" to people who did not ask.

I also discovered something useful about my body: my max heart rate is around 204 BPM and my VO2max is naturally high. This is mostly genetic luck. It doesn't mean I'm fast. It means my ceiling is higher than my current fitness would suggest, which is a polite way of saying I have a lot of room to improve. But it also means that if I actually train properly, the gains are there to be had.

So the last eight months have been structured blocks. Run training zones calibrated to pace and heart rate. Plyometrics for ground contact time. Single-leg machine work to build connective tissue tolerance because three years of upper body lifting left my tendons completely unprepared for high-impact intervals. Slow eccentric hamstring curls as Achilles insurance. Dead bugs as a breathing intervention because my calisthenics background taught me to brace my core like I was about to get punched, which is great for a planche and terrible for running at threshold. A 2:2 breathing cadence enforced on every tempo session. Pre-session carb loading on a caloric deficit because running VO2max intervals in a glycogen-depleted state made me nauseous enough to reconsider my life choices.

It sounds like a lot written out. It is a lot. But each piece exists because something went wrong and I had to fix it. The shoulder injury that killed barbell pressing. The nausea at 197 BPM that meant my ventilatory system couldn't keep up with my legs. The tendon pain that meant my connective tissue was two training ages behind my aerobic fitness. Every protocol in my training plan is a scar from a lesson learned.

---

Which brings us to now. I'm training for a spot on Team Canada's Age Group Aquathlon team, targeting the 2027 World Championships in Edmonton. The race is a 2.5 km run, 1 km swim, 2.5 km run. I need to hit 3\:15 per km on the first leg and 4\:05 per km on the second. Eight months ago those numbers would have hospitalized me. Now they're within range.

I'm also doing this on top of a full UofT course load and a full-time position at Mozilla, where I work on Firefox. The training happens in the evenings, in a condo gym and at Hart House, squeezed between lectures and pull requests. Some weeks the schedule works. Some weeks I'm doing VO2max intervals at 9 PM after a day of debugging privacy features for 250 million users and then waking up for a 9 AM lecture. It is not a sustainable lifestyle in any clinical sense of the word. But nobody asked.

If you had told grade 10 me, the kid who couldn't return a volleyball serve, that he would one day be training for a national team while holding down a full course load and a full-time engineering job, he would have assumed you were making fun of him. Which is fair. It does sound made up.

But here we are. The kid who hated phys ed is now the guy with a spreadsheet full of training zones and a cabinet full of protein powder. The pages that follow are what that looks like week by week.
